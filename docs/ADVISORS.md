# Storey — Advisor Plan

A working list of who Storey wants on the advisor bench, why, and how to track
the outreach. Update this in place as conversations happen.

---

## Why advisors

An advisor opens **doors, judgement and credibility** you don't have yet — at
roughly 1/100th the cost of a hire. For a founder building a B2B SaaS solo,
two or three well-chosen advisors compound more than another developer would.

What you actually want from each: 30–60 minutes a month, intros to people you
can't reach cold, and *honest* push-back on decisions.

## What to offer (norms, not rules)

- **Time:** 30–60 minutes a month, plus ad-hoc messages. Set the expectation up front.
- **Equity (typical):** **0.1–0.5%** vesting over **2 years** with a 3-month cliff.
  - 0.1–0.25% — light advisor, intros + occasional call
  - 0.25–0.5% — heavy advisor, deeply involved
- **Cash:** usually zero for early-stage; sometimes a small monthly stipend for
  heavy advisors.
- **Paperwork:** a one-page **FAST Agreement** (Founder Advisor Standard
  Template from Founder Institute, free) — or the equivalent under Indian law,
  drafted by your CA / lawyer once you register the entity.
- **Get the entity registered first** — you can't grant equity from an
  unregistered business. (This is on the main TODO.)

## Outreach playbook

1. **Warm beats cold, every time.** Use LinkedIn, your contacts, school/college
   networks. Ask for a 15-minute intro call, not "to advise" yet.
2. **The first message is short.** *"Building a site-operations app for NE-India
   contractors. Real product, live in beta with 2 testers. Would value 15 mins
   of your perspective on [specific topic they care about]. Can I send a link?"*
3. **Lead the meeting.** Have a one-page brief (what you're building, who for,
   what's working, what you'd want from them). Don't ask them what to do — show
   what you're doing and where you want their judgement.
4. **Ask after 2–3 calls**, not on the first. By then they've already invested
   time; the formal ask is small.
5. **Track every conversation in the table above.** A "no" today is a "yes" in
   18 months.

---

## What NOT to do

- Don't offer equity in the first message.
- Don't hand out advisor slots cheaply — every diluted % matters, and an
  inactive advisor is a long-term tax.
- Don't take advice without checking it against your own conviction. Advisors
  give context, *you* decide.
- Don't recruit advisors for status. A famous name who never replies is worse
  than an unknown name who does.
